# During-Exercise Fueling — TrainingCoach (2026)

## Overview

During-exercise carbohydrate intake is the primary nutritional intervention for endurance performance in sessions lasting >60 minutes. Recent research (2020-2025) has significantly updated recommendations, particularly around glucose:fructose ratios and maximum intake rates.

## Dual Transporter Model

Carbohydrate absorption during exercise is limited by intestinal transporter capacity. Two independent transporters handle different sugars:

| Transporter | Location | Sugar | Saturation Rate |
|-------------|----------|-------|-----------------|
| **SGLT1** | Small intestine | Glucose (+ maltodextrin, starch) | ~60 g/hr |
| **GLUT5** | Small intestine | Fructose | ~30-36 g/hr |

### Why Multiple Transportable Carbohydrates (MTC)?

Using glucose alone, absorption saturates at ~60 g/hr. Unabsorbed carbohydrate in the gut causes GI distress (bloating, cramping, diarrhea). By combining glucose + fructose, athletes use both transporters simultaneously, increasing total absorption to ~90+ g/hr with lower GI distress.

```
Glucose-only:     SGLT1 → max ~60 g/hr
Glucose+Fructose: SGLT1 (~54 g/hr) + GLUT5 (~36 g/hr) → max ~90 g/hr
```

## Glucose:Fructose Ratio — 2025 Update

### Old Recommendation (pre-2020): 2:1

The original Jentjens & Jeukendrup (2004) studies established a 2:1 glucose:fructose ratio as optimal. This was based on early dose-finding studies.

### Current Recommendation (2023-2025): 1:0.8

Recent research has shifted the optimal ratio closer to equimolar:

```
Old ratio:     2:1   (glucose:fructose)  →  67% glucose, 33% fructose
New ratio:     1:0.8 (glucose:fructose)  →  56% glucose, 44% fructose
```

**Key evidence for the ratio update:**

- **Rowlands et al. (2022)**: Demonstrated that a 1:0.8 ratio improved exogenous carbohydrate oxidation by ~15% compared to 2:1 during prolonged cycling
- **King et al. (2022)**: Showed 0.8:1 fructose:glucose (equivalent to 1:0.8 glucose:fructose) maximized oxidation rates and reduced GI symptoms
- **Podlogar & Wallis (2022)**: Review supporting higher fructose ratios for improved oxidation and tolerance
- **Hearris et al. (2022)**: Confirmed the GLUT5 transporter has higher capacity than previously estimated, supporting increased fructose proportion

**Why more fructose?** Early studies underestimated GLUT5 capacity. Fructose is metabolized primarily by the liver into glucose and lactate, providing a secondary fuel source that spares muscle glycogen. Higher fructose ratios improve total oxidation while reducing the osmotic load on SGLT1.

## Intake Rate Guidelines

### Evidence-Based Ceiling: 90 g/hr

For most athletes, 90 g/hr of mixed glucose:fructose represents the practical maximum with acceptable GI tolerance:

```
90 g/hr at 1:0.8 ratio:
  Glucose (maltodextrin): ~50 g/hr
  Fructose:              ~40 g/hr
```

### Tolerable Maximum: 120 g/hr

Recent elite-level studies (Jeukendrup 2024, Stellingwerff 2024) have explored intakes up to 120 g/hr:

- Tolerable in trained, gut-adapted athletes
- May provide marginal performance benefit over 90 g/hr in events >3 hours
- **Not proven to be superior to 90 g/hr** — diminishing returns with increased GI risk
- Requires systematic gut training over 4-6 weeks

| Intake Rate | Evidence Level | Recommendation |
|------------|---------------|----------------|
| 30-60 g/hr | Strong | Minimum for events 1-2.5 hours |
| 60-90 g/hr | Strong | Optimal for events 2.5-5 hours |
| 90 g/hr | Strong | Evidence-based ceiling for most athletes |
| 90-120 g/hr | Emerging | Tolerable with gut training; not proven better |
| > 120 g/hr | Insufficient | Not recommended; high GI distress risk |

### By Event Duration

| Duration | Carb Intake | Type |
|----------|-------------|------|
| < 60 min | None needed (mouth rinse optional) | N/A |
| 60 - 90 min | 30 g/hr | Glucose or any |
| 90 - 150 min | 60 g/hr | Glucose + fructose |
| 150 - 300 min | 60 - 90 g/hr | Glucose + fructose (1:0.8) |
| > 300 min | 80 - 90 g/hr | Glucose + fructose (1:0.8) |

## Timing and Practical Delivery

### Timing Strategy

```
Pre-exercise (2-3 hours before): 1-3 g/kg carb meal
Pre-exercise (30 min before):    Small carb top-up (20-30g) — optional
During exercise:                 Begin fueling within first 15-20 minutes
                                 Consume every 15-20 minutes (not all at once)
Post-exercise:                   See recovery-science.md
```

### Delivery Forms

| Form | Pros | Cons | Best For |
|------|------|------|----------|
| Sports drink | Easy to consume, combines hydration | Limited concentration | Cycling, running |
| Gels (25-30g each) | Concentrated, portable | Needs water; GI risk if undiluted | Running, cycling |
| Chews/gummies | Palatable variety | Slower absorption; chewing effort | Cycling, long ultra |
| Real food (rice cakes, bars) | Palatability in long events | Slow gastric emptying | Ultra-endurance |
| Drink mix powder | Customizable concentration | Requires planning | Cycling (bottles) |

### Concentration Guidelines

```
Sports drink: 6-8% carbohydrate solution (60-80g per liter)
Higher concentrations (>8%) slow gastric emptying and increase GI distress
Hypertonic solutions (>10%) should be avoided during exercise
```

## Hydration

### Fluid Intake

```
Target: 200 - 250 ml every 15 - 20 minutes
Rate:   ~500 - 750 ml/hr (adjust for heat, humidity, sweat rate)
```

Do not over-hydrate. Exercise-associated hyponatremia (EAH) from excessive fluid intake is more dangerous than mild dehydration. Body weight loss of 2-3% during exercise is normal and does not impair performance in most conditions.

### Sodium

```
Target: 600 - 1000 mg sodium per hour
Higher end: hot conditions, heavy sweaters, events > 4 hours
```

| Sweat Rate | Sodium Concentration | Hourly Sodium Need |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Light sweater | 400 mg/L | 300 - 500 mg/hr |
| Average sweater | 700 mg/L | 500 - 800 mg/hr |
| Heavy / salty sweater | 1000+ mg/L | 800 - 1200 mg/hr |

Sodium sources: sports drinks (~300-500mg/L), salt capsules (200-250mg each), salted food.

## Gut Training

Athletes can increase carbohydrate absorption capacity through systematic gut training:

1. Practice race-day nutrition in training (minimum 1x/week)
2. Gradually increase intake rate over 4-6 weeks
3. Train with the same products planned for racing
4. Start at 40-50 g/hr and increase by 10 g/hr per week
5. High-carb diets during training increase SGLT1 transporter expression

## Sport-Specific Considerations

### Cycling
- Easiest to fuel (upright-ish position, can carry bottles/food)
- Aim for the upper end of intake ranges
- Use a mix of drink, gels, and solid food for variety in long rides

### Running
- Harder to fuel (impact, jostling, reduced GI blood flow)
- Lower intake rates may be needed (start at 30-60 g/hr)
- Gels + water at aid stations; drink mixes in handheld bottles
- Practice in training to avoid race-day GI issues

### Swimming
- Fueling during pool training: between sets, use drink bottles on deck
- Open water: feed stations or carried gels (less practical)
- Generally shorter duration; fueling matters most for >90 min OW swims
